Ruby-Throated Hummingbirds Migration Project

Here is an interactive map showing Ruby-Throated Hummingbirds migration by month over the course of 2023:

The Ruby-Throated Hummingbird is the only hummingbird inhabiting the Eastern United States. They also inhabit southern Canada and as far west as Oklahoma in the south, and Alberta Canada in the north. During migration, these birds fly south across the Gulf of Mexico and winter in southern Mexico and Central America. From the map we see that the birds begin to migrate in August and most are fully south by November. They then begin to travel back north in March and April with most returning north by May. The birds in the Northern range migrate south first and come back north the latest.

I think we can all see where the Ruby-Throated Hummingbird gets its name! The Ruby-Throated Hummingbird can beat its wings 50 times a second, allowing it to hover perfectly stationary. Its nest is also interesting, built from plant matter woven together from spiderwebs and lichen, just big enough for the mother to fit inside. A full-grown adult hummingbird only weighs 2–3 grams on average, the same wieght as a penny! Although they weigh practically nothing, they still migrate from as far as Southern Canada to Mexico. Some take single 500-mile journey across the Gulf of Mexico! To prepare, they double their fat reserves and take advantage of favorable winds.

Interestingly, the map I created shows hummingbirds farther north in Canada than existing range maps suggest. Could this indicate that the Ruby-Throated Hummingbird is expanding its range northward due to climate change? Or could this pattern simply reflect regional aggregation of the data? Further investigation would be needed, but it makes for an interesting research question!

NDVI & Race Inequality Analysis in Philadelphia (2020 Census + MODIS NDVI)

This project analyzes vegetation greenness (NDVI) across all census tracts in Philadelphia and compares it to racial demographics using 2020 U.S. Census data. Satellite NDVI values were computed using three 2021 MODIS scenes and averaged to produce a mean NDVI for each tract.

The goal of this analysis was to explore whether predominantly White, Black, Latino, or Asian neighborhoods in Philadelphia experience different levels of vegetation cover — an important environmental justice indicator.

Methodology

  • Loaded 2010 Census tract geometries for Philadelphia County.
  • Downloaded 2020 Census PL94 race data via the Census API.
  • Loaded and processed MODIS NDVI rasters (16-day composites).
  • Computed tract-level mean NDVI using zonal statistics.
  • Generated correlation plots for:
    • % White vs NDVI
    • % Black vs NDVI
    • % Asian vs NDVI
    • % Latino (Hispanic) vs NDVI
  • Compared groups using a boxplot of majority-race tracts.

Interpretation: NDVI values generally ranged from 0.25–0.55 across Philadelphia, which is typical for an urban–suburban environment with a mix of tree canopy, parks, sidewalks, and built infrastructure. Across racial groups, the differences in NDVI were small and correlations were close to zero, suggesting no strong relationship between racial composition and vegetation greenness at the census-tract scale.

The boxplot comparing majority-White, majority-Black, majority-Latino, and majority-Asian neighborhoods shows modest variation but no clear environmental disparity in vegetation cover according to this dataset and time window. Additional factors such as income, zoning, tree canopy data, or longitudinal NDVI could provide deeper insight.
